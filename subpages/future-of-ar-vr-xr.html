What Are Potential Future Applications For VR, AR, and XR Technologies?

Word Count: 325

Before discussing the future potentials of VR, AR, and XR technologies, it's essential to specify which of these technologies are. Virtual reality (VR) is a three-dimensional simulated environment, usually achieved through technologies such as goggles, headsets, gloves, and/or body suits (Lonwood). Augmented Reality (AR) uses "camera-equippad hardware such as smart glasses" to add digital elements into your physical space (Hayes). Extended Reality (XR) is an umbrella term for technologies which combine the physical world and digital environments, like VR, AR, and Mixed Reality (MR) ("Extended Reality").

Mental health professionals can use VR for treatments. For example, if a mental health professional suggests that a patient undergo exposure therapy, then VR could be a safe way to do that. A VR environment could simulate a tall building, and the patient could attempt to overpower their fear of heights by being exposed to virtual danger. As it's a VR environment, it's realistic, but the patient also knows they have the right to withdraw whenever they feel like they have to stop. Whereas if the patient were to go to a tall building, it may not be as easy to leave the experience, as there are often crowds and other unpredictable variables.

An AR application could be used for navigation in workspaces, such as warehouses, by workers managing inventory. AR tech could work by a customer placing a pickup order, then an employee would receive a notification on their AR glasses, similar to what Microsoft's HoloLens aims to be. The glasses would use ultra-wideband access points throughout the warehouse to triangulate their location based on signal strength. Then, once it's established location, it would visually guide the employee to the item and even highlight it on the shelf.

These examples represent only a small subset of potential XR applications, and engaging critically with such technologies as consumers is essential, as they are generally developed to increase efficiency, accessibility, and quality of life rather than to complicate everyday experience.
